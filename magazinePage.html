On 23 JULY, Daniel Radcliffe turns 20/ There are no huge milestones waiting for him - he can already drink, he's had sex, he has exercised hid democratic right to vote - but thtre will be the small matter of the beginning of the rest of his life. Having served as Boy Wizard Laureate for eight years now, his time's nearly up. The sixth film in the massively successful adaptations of JK Rowling's Harry Potter books, Harry Potter and The Half-Blood Prince, comes out in July. He is already in the middle of shooting the two-part final film, Harry Potter And The Deathy Hallows, which will wrap next year. Part One will come out that summer, Part Two follow in 2011.

Right now, he has other things to worry about. It's a mild Sunday in April, and Radcliffe is in a studio in north London where a debate is taking place between the stylist, photographer, make-up artict and piblicist about exactly how he should be beaten up. Should the black eye be more purple? Would a trickle of blood from the mostril be too much? And if you were going to coer up a lightning-strike scar on a forehead, which way should the plaster lean? The question of why anyone would want to beat him up, we'll come to later.

In the middle of it all, the diminutive actor, a modest 5th 5i, is singing a king to Tom Lehrer's 1950 ditty "The Elements", which is playing from his iPod via the studio's stereo. This basically means, like soe sort of gnod-natured hostage trying not to lose his mind, he is reciting the periodic table. It's the peculiar paradox of being a cerebrity: he's both the centre of everyone's attention and the one whose opinion is least required. To misappropriate a term from recent political satire In The Loop, he's "room meat".

"I live my life in a sort of daze," says Radcliffe, contentedly. "I go where I'm told and answer questions. It's quite a nice way of living." The shoot has finished and we have moved tho the studio's darkened, empty bar, where he settles into a leather chair with his umpreenth Diet Coke.
